# Doctoral Proposal Conventions

## The rhetorical sequence

Most doctoral proposals, regardless of discipline, follow the same underlying logic:

```
Research object
  → problem context (why this object, why now)
    → gap in existing scholarship
      → answerable research questions
        → methods matched to questions
          → significance (what this clarifies)
            → feasibility and plan
```

Write in this order. Do not start with methods before the gap is established, and do not claim significance before showing what the gap is.

## Section logic

**Background and context**
Move from field → specific site or object → transformation pressure → theoretical need. Introduce the research object before listing materials or methods.

**Research questions and content**
State the questions first. Then describe the chapter or content units that answer them. The structure of the paper should be derivable from the questions.

**Literature review**
Classify scholarship by what each line of work *explains*, then name what it leaves unexplained. The gap should be legible from the review — not added as a conclusion at the end.

**Methods**
Each method must specify: what it analyzes, what evidence it uses, and why it is necessary for *this* research question. A method without a question is overhead.

**Innovation and significance**
State what shifts: object of study, method, material, or explanatory frame. Significance is what the research will *clarify*, not a general claim that the topic is important.

**Research plan and feasibility**
Sequence by research dependency, not only by calendar. Show that necessary access, materials, and expertise are in place or have a concrete path to being in place.

## Style rules

**On citation placement**: do not end paragraphs with citation piles. Embed citations near the specific claim they support.

**On abbreviations**: spell out every abbreviation, institution name, and technical term on first use. One short appositive phrase is usually enough: "Luang Prabang, a UNESCO World Heritage city in northern Laos."

**On verb choice**: prefer synthesis verbs: "demonstrates," "suggests," "reveals," "constitutes," "indicates." Avoid "proves" (overstates) and "mentions" (understates).

**On alarm vocabulary**: avoid terms like "urgent," "endangered," "crisis," or "rescue" unless directly supported by a cited source. Unsupported alarm language weakens credibility.

**On abstraction**: do not repeat the same abstract noun chain in consecutive sentences. After writing "spatial structure and craft transmission," the next sentence should name a specific element — a material, an actor, a process, a spatial feature.

**On figures**: a figure should appear after the paragraph that establishes its analytical role, not before. The reader should know why they are looking before they look.

## Distinguishing what you know from what you plan

A proposal is not a completed thesis. Be explicit about what is established, what is planned, and what is contingent:

- "Existing scholarship shows…" — established
- "This research will examine…" — planned
- "Subject to fieldwork, this chapter will…" — contingent

Do not write planned fieldwork results as though they are completed. Do not write "this study finds" when the study has not yet been conducted.

## Common structural mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Methods section that could apply to any topic | Tie each method explicitly to a research question |
| Literature review that ends with "therefore this topic is important" | The gap should be visible throughout the review, not just in a closing sentence |
| Innovation claimed without specifying what shifts | Name the specific shift: new object, new material, new method, new frame |
| Research questions stated after the content outline | Questions come first; content outline answers them |
| Significance stated as "this fills a gap" | Explain what understanding becomes possible once the gap is filled |
